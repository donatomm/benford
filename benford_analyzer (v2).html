Statistical Conformity Test

Applying Benford's Law for spotting doctored data

Are the books cooked? Did these votes actually happen? Did this scientist fake the data? Is this earnings report honest? Is this claim real or invented? Was this country's data fabricated?

What is Benford's Law?

In any large, naturally occurring dataset — invoices, population figures, stock prices, river lengths — the number 1 appears as the first digit about 30% of the time. The number 2 about 17%, and so on down to 9 at just 4.6%. This is not intuitive, but it is remarkably consistent across unrelated domains. Datasets that were fabricated or heavily manipulated tend to break this pattern, which makes it a useful fraud detection signal.

What is the MAD score?

MAD stands for Mean Absolute Deviation. It measures the average gap between what your data shows and what Benford's Law predicts, across all nine first digits. A score near zero means your data closely follows the expected pattern. A high score means it diverges — which may or may not indicate a problem depending on the nature of your dataset.

What do the colors mean?

Green — close (<0.006) The digit conforms tightly. No concern.
Blue — acceptable (0.006–0.012) Minor deviation within normal variation.
Amber — marginal (0.012–0.015) Worth noting. May warrant a closer look.
Red — non-conformity (>0.015) Statistically significant deviation. Investigate.

What to look for in the chart

Each colored bar is your data. The gray bar behind it is Benford's prediction. If your colored bars are consistently taller or shorter than the gray ones — especially for digit 1 — that is the signal. Digit 1 being under-represented is a classic fabrication pattern: people inventing numbers tend to avoid starting with 1 because it feels too small. Digits 5–7 being over-represented is a rounding pattern: people round to "nice" numbers.

Important caveats

Benford's Law works best on datasets with at least 100–300 numbers spanning several orders of magnitude (e.g. mixing hundreds, thousands, millions). It does not apply to constrained ranges (e.g. ages 1–99, percentages, assigned ID numbers) or datasets from a single order of magnitude. Deviation alone is not proof of fraud — it is a signal to investigate further.
